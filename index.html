Tatujuko is a minimalist constructed language featuring a unique tripartite writing system inspired by Japanese and Korean scripts. With only 120 functional words and no verb conjugations, it achieves remarkable expressiveness through context and compound meanings.

üí≠ Philosophy

Tatujuko is built on three core principles that guide both its structure and usage:

üî≠ Observation

Describing what is seen, heard, and experienced directly. Language as a tool for witnessing reality.

üîó Connection

Focus on relationships between concepts, beings, and ideas. Everything exists in relation.

üåä Flow

Expressing process and transformation over fixed states. Reality is fluid, language should be too.

‚úçÔ∏è Writing System

Tatujuko uses three distinct writing modes, inspired by Japanese (kanji + kana) and Korean (hangul) writing systems. Each mode serves a specific function and creates instant visual grammar recognition.

Complete Alphabet & Logograms

The complete writing system showing all phonetic characters and semantic logograms

üé≠ Three Writing Modes

üî§

1. Phonetic Alphabet

Individual sound characters written separately. Building blocks of the language. Used by beginners and for uncommon words.

üé®

2. Semantic Logograms

Combined phonetic characters compressed into artistic unified forms. Used for common/important words. Creates visual beauty and efficiency.

„Ä∞Ô∏è

3. Verb Flow Script

Continuous flowing characters without breaks. Represents action and movement. Instantly distinguishes verbs from nouns.

Visual Grammar

The writing style instantly shows word function:

  • Nouns ‚Üí Logograms or separate phonetic characters (static, defined)
  • Verbs ‚Üí Continuous flowing script (dynamic, action)
  • Particles ‚Üí Separate phonetic characters (grammatical glue)

Example: The Language's Name

Tatujuko can be written as three logograms: [Ta][Tu][Juko] - the language naming itself in its own script!

üìê Grammar

Word Order: SVO (Subject-Verb-Object)

Tatujuko uses simple, consistent word order:

Sen aji kotsi
I see water (Subject-Verb-Object)
Li maji malu
You eat flower
Na jinatsu Tatujuko
They speak Tatujuko

Questions: Using Naki

Two positions for the question marker Naki:

Naki li aji?
What do you see? (Naki at start = content question)
Li aji kotsi naki?
Do you see water? (Naki at end = yes/no question)

Negation: Using Kusa

Place Kusa before the verb to negate:

Sen kusa aji li
I don't see you (I no-see you)
Kusa, sen aji kotsi
No, I see water (Kusa as response)

Possession: Using Tso

Pattern: Possessed + Tso + Possessor

Kotsi tso sen
Water of me (my water)
Pola tso li
Tree of you (your tree)

No Conjugations! üéâ

Verbs never change form. Time is understood from context:

Sen aji kotsi
I see water / I saw water / I will see water

Adjectives Follow Nouns

Kotsi posu
Water hot (hot water)
Pola jona lino
Tree big beautiful (big beautiful tree)

Connecting with E (and) & Ik (or)

Sen aji sola e luna
I see sun and moon
Li otsa mi mano ik peso
You go to land or ocean

üí¨ Example Sentences

Basic Expressions

Sen aji li
I see you
Sen kemi li
I love you
Ner, sen kemi Tatujuko!
Yes, I love Tatujuko!

With Adjectives

Sen aji pola jona
I see big tree
Kotsi posu lino
Water hot beautiful (beautiful hot water)
Sola kisa e luna lino
Sun good and moon beautiful

Complex Sentences

Sen aji sola lino imaji li
I see beautiful sun with you
Ki otsa mi wenu e ki aji tenu lino
We go to mountain and we see beautiful sky
Li araju Tatujuko e li jinatsu imaji sen naki?
You know Tatujuko and you speak with me, yes?

Poetic Expressions

Sen kotsi
I am water / I water (philosophical: I am fluid, I flow)
Kotsi aji sen
Water sees me (the world observes the observer)
Sola e luna tso ta tenu
Sun and moon of the sky (the celestial pair)
Ki senu tso ta sola
We are seeds of the sun (we are children of light)

Sample Conversation

Person A:

Sen aji sola lino. Li aji naki?
I see beautiful sun. Do you see?

Person B:

Ner! Sen aji sola e luna. Atso lino.
Yes! I see sun and moon. They are beautiful.

Person A:

Ki otsa mi wenu naki?
Shall we go to mountain?

Person B:

Ner, sen jitsu otsa mi wenu. Sen kemi tenu lino.
Yes, I want to go to mountain. I love beautiful sky.

üìñ Learning Path

üå±

Beginner (Weeks 1-2)

  • Master phonetic alphabet (14 sounds)
  • Learn 6 pronouns
  • Learn 10 basic verbs
  • Practice SVO word order
  • Learn numbers 1-10
  • Write everything phonetically

Goal: Simple sentences like "Sen aji li" (I see you)

üåø

Intermediate (Weeks 3-6)

  • Learn major logograms (Ta, Tu, Juko, Sen, Li, etc.)
  • Master all 11 particles
  • Learn 30+ common verbs and nouns
  • Practice writing verbs in flowing script
  • Build multi-clause sentences
  • Learn numbers to 100

Goal: Descriptive sentences with adjectives and particles

üå≥

Advanced (Months 2-3)

  • Internalize all logograms
  • Master all 120 core words
  • Write fluidly mixing all three scripts
  • Create philosophical expressions
  • Write original poems and stories
  • Contribute to vocabulary expansion

Goal: Fluent expression and creative writing

üí° Learning Tips

  • Start phonetically: Don't worry about logograms at first - write everything in phonetic characters
  • Learn in context: Practice with full sentences, not isolated words
  • Think broadly: Remember words have flexible meanings - "kotsi" isn't just water, it's liquid/fluid/flow
  • Practice daily: Even 10 minutes a day builds fluency faster than long weekly sessions
  • Create content: Write a journal entry, compose a poem, translate a song - make it yours!
